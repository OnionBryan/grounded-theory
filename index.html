Foundation

The Logic of Grounded Theory Coding

The first analytic turn in our grounded theory journey brings us to coding. Grounded theory coding requires us to stop and ask analytic questions of the data we have gathered. These questions not only further our understanding of studied life but also help us direct subsequent data-gathering toward the analytic issues we are defining. Coding consists of at least two phases: initial and focused coding (Ch. 6).

Through coding, you define what is happening in the data and begin to grapple with what it means. The codes take form together as elements of a nascent theory that directs further data-gathering. By careful attending to coding, you begin weaving two major threads: generalizable theoretical statements that transcend specific times and places, and contextual analyses of actions and events.

As Star (2007, p. 80) writes, "a code sets up a relationship with your data, and with your respondents." Coding means naming segments of data with a short name that simultaneously summarizes and accounts for each piece of data. Your codes show how you select, separate, and sort data and begin an analytic accounting of them.

Provisional

Initial codes are temporary. You remain open to other analytic possibilities and may reword earlier codes to improve fit. Compelling codes capture the phenomenon and grab the reader—like changing “hearing about illness” to “receiving second-hand news,” which implies diminished status and emotional charge (p. 118).

Comparative

Compare data with data, data with codes, and codes with codes. The “constant comparative method” (Glaser & Strauss, 1967) establishes analytic distinctions at every level. It is the engine that drives discovery.

Grounded

The logic of grounded theory differs from quantitative logic which applies preconceived categories. Grounded theorists create codes by defining what we see in the data. Codes emerge as you scrutinize your data and define meanings within it (p. 114).

Two Main Phases

Phase 1

Initial Coding

Focused Coding

Constructing Codes

We construct our codes because we are actively naming data—even when we believe our codes form a perfect fit with actions and events in the studied world. We may think our codes capture the empirical reality. Yet it is our view: we choose the words that constitute our codes (p. 115).

The coding process is interactive. We interact with our participants and subsequently interact with them again many times over through studying their statements and observed actions. We must dig into our data to interpret participants’ tacit meanings. Close attention to coding helps us to do that.

Language plays a crucial role. No researcher is neutral because language confers form and meaning on observed realities. Our codes arise from the languages, meanings, and perspectives through which we learn about the empirical world. Coding impels us to make our participants’ language problematic—and should inspire us to examine hidden assumptions in our own language as well (p. 115).

Entering an interactive analytic space. As you conduct grounded theory coding, you enter an interactive space that pulls you deeper into the data. The subjective merges with what appears to be objective as you grapple with understanding this world. Your codes can shift, change, or transform your relationship with your data (p. 116).

Star (2007, p. 84) notes that grounded theory codes are “transitional objects.” They crystallize what we learn while studying our data and connect fragments of data with the analytic abstraction that we accord to them. Grounded theory coding generates the bones of your analysis. Theoretical centrality and integration will assemble these bones into a working skeleton.

The First Step

Initial Coding: Staying Open

When grounded theorists conduct initial coding, we remain open to exploring whatever theoretical possibilities we can discern. Through comparing data with data, we learn what participants view as problematic and begin to treat it analytically. During initial coding, we ask:

“What is this data a study of?” (Glaser, 1978, p. 57)
What do the data suggest? Pronounce? Leave unsaid?
From whose point of view?
What theoretical category does this specific datum indicate?

Initial coding should stick closely to the data. Try to see actions in each segment rather than applying pre-existing categories. Coding for actions reduces tendencies to make conceptual leaps and to adopt extant theories before doing the necessary analytic work (p. 116).

Three Scales of Initial Coding

Word-by-Word

Particularly helpful with documents, Internet data, or ephemera. Forces you to attend to the structure and flow of words, and how both affect the sense you make of them (p. 124).

Line-by-Line

The workhorse of initial coding. A heuristic device to bring the researcher into the data, interact with them, and study each fragment. Defines implicit meanings and actions, gives directions to explore, spurs comparisons, and suggests emergent links between processes (p. 124).

Incident-by-Incident

Compare incident with incident, then incidents to your conceptualization. Works well with observational data. Making comparisons gives you clues to follow. Compare and code similar events, then define subtle patterns and significant processes (p. 128).

A code for coding (p. 120): Remain open. Stay close to the data. Keep your codes simple and precise. Construct short codes. Preserve actions. Compare data with data. Move quickly through the data.

Core Technique

Coding for Topics vs. Coding with Gerunds

Glaser (1978) shows how coding with gerunds helps you detect processes and stick to the data. Think of the difference: describing versus description, stating versus statement, leading versus leader. We gain a strong sense of action and sequence with gerunds. The nouns turn these actions into topics (p. 120).

The Ominous Moment (Box 5.7, New)

Charmaz compares two incidents of “comprehending the ominous moment”—Teresa learning she could lose her voice, and Gail’s injury during gymnastics. Despite vastly different contexts, both share properties: perceiving rapid successive cues, realizing the meaning of a pivotal point of self, instantaneous awareness despite shock, shattering the self, and immediate understanding of consequences (p. 130).

Participants’ Language

In Vivo Codes

Grounded theorists refer to codes of participants’ special terms as in vivo codes. In the 2nd edition (p. 134), Charmaz identifies four kinds that prove useful—though many examples span or blend these categories. The 1st edition (2006) also highlighted borrowed metaphors and condensed experience as analytically productive forms:

“Breaking the ice”
1. Condensed Meanings
Terms everyone “knows” that flag condensed but significant meanings. Ziegahn & Hinchman’s (1999) codes—“breaking the ice,” “figuring out how to help,” “trying to understand”—provided form and coherence to their study of college tutors. They use gerunds to portray problems the tutors wrestled with (p. 134).
“Supernormal”
2. Innovative Terms
A participant’s unique term capturing meanings or experience. A young doctor with severe diabetes described pursuing “supernormal” identity goals—not merely managing his condition but excelling beyond peers. Once grasped, Charmaz saw this process in other participants’ actions and stated intentions (p. 135).
“Black Knight”
3. Insider Shorthand
Terms reflecting a particular group’s perspective. Morrill’s (1995) corporate executives used “black knight,” “flying low,” and “vaporizing” with specific in-house meanings evoking metaphors of combat and violation (p. 135).
“The ‘buck’ stops with me”
4. Crystallized Actions/Concerns
Statements that crystallize participants’ actions or concerns. New to 2nd ed. Richardson (2010) used this in vivo code as his article title and wove it into his analysis of Irish men’s health policy, linking men’s experience with national policy tensions (p. 134).
“Hitting bottom”
Condensed Meaning
General terms everyone “knows” that flag condensed but significant meanings. The term “battered woman,” for instance, was shown by Loseke (1992) to carry specific, exclusionary assumptions by claims-makers about who qualifies for services—revealing how language shapes policy.
“Making a comeback”
Borrowed Metaphor
By borrowing a term from once-successful celebrities, a participant defined his stance toward chronic illness. The term simultaneously reveals identity goals, social context, and the performative nature of illness management—reaching far beyond individual psychology (p. 135).
“Good days / Bad days”
Condensed Experience
Many chronically ill participants used these phrases. Charmaz sought the condensed meanings and actions these terms covered—discovering how time structures experience when illness is unpredictable. “Taking one day at a time” similarly revealed shared orientations toward temporality (p. 135).
Caution: In vivo codes may be catchy, but they do not stand on their own. Like any other code, they must be subjected to comparative and analytic treatment and integrated into the theory (p. 134).
New Section

Transforming Data into Codes

Coding relies on having solid data. How and what you record affects what you have to code. Charmaz emphasizes that coding full interview transcriptions gives you ideas and understandings you otherwise miss (p. 136).

Full Transcription

Coding full transcriptions brings you to a deeper level of understanding. Rich data from first reading and coding need not be the final one—return and recode old data as new ideas spark (p. 136).

From Notes

Coding from notes might give a wider view, but can lead to going around rather than into the phenomenon. An overriding emphasis on plausibility risks constructing superficial analyses (p. 136).

Concluding thought (p. 137): Grounded theory coding is part work but also part play. We play with the data. We become involved and learn from them. Theoretical playfulness allows us to try out ideas and see where they may lead. Initial coding is the first part of the adventure that enables you to make the leap from concrete events to theoretical insight.
Watch Out

Reducing Problems in Coding

Researchers are enjoined to avoid forcing data into preconceived codes and categories. These pitfalls are common and often invisible.

Forcing Extant Theories

Students adopt ideas like “risk,” “habitus,” “discourses of power” to apply rather than interrogate. “I’m going to use Marx—or Foucault—or Harding.” Such applications preclude your ideas from emerging. The openness of initial coding should spark thinking and allow new ideas to emerge (p. 117).

Coding People as Types

Assigning types to people casts them with static labels. You make them one-dimensional although behavior may change as events change. Such coding freezes people in time and space and erases defining variation (p. 117).

Accepting Views Uncritically

Becoming so immersed you accept respondents’ worldviews without question. If you had already accepted the same assumptions, you risk reproducing them. Being critical means asking questions about your data—not being critical of participants (p. 127).

Static Topical Coding

Picking up general terms like “experience” or “event” tells you little about meaning or action. Qualify general terms. Make codes fit the data rather than forcing data to fit them. See Box 5.3 vs. 5.4 contrast above (p. 120).

Conceptual Leaps

Jumping from data to sweeping theoretical claims without the necessary analytic work. Coding with gerunds and for actions curbs this. Build step by step from the ground up (p. 117).

Overreliance on Notes vs. Transcripts

New emphasis in 2nd ed.: few grounded theorists advocate coding from notes rather than transcriptions. Gary Fine’s (1993) experiment showed even trained ethnographers rapidly forgot details. Coding full transcriptions gives you ideas you otherwise miss (p. 136).

Raising Abstraction

From Initial to Focused, Axial & Theoretical

After initial coding, you select the most significant or frequent codes to sift through large amounts of data. This is focused coding—selective and conceptual. It requires decisions about which initial codes make the most analytic sense to categorize your data incisively and completely.

Not entirely linear. Some respondents or events will make explicit what was implicit in earlier statements. An “Aha!” moment may prompt you to study your earlier data afresh and return to explore topics that had been glossed over.

Focused Coding

Select the most frequent or significant initial codes and test them against extensive data. Move across interviews and observations; compare people’s experiences, actions, and interpretations. The codes condense data and provide an analytic handle. Example: initial codes like “accusing daughter of not caring” and “explaining lack of disclosure” might synthesize into the focused code “Assessing potential losses and risks of disclosing.”

Axial Coding

Strauss & Corbin’s technique for relating categories to subcategories. Specifies properties, dimensions, conditions, actions/interactions, and consequences. Provides a frame: useful for those who prefer preset structure, but potentially limiting if applied rigidly. Charmaz cautions it can cast a “technological overlay” on the data.

Theoretical Coding

Conceptualizes how substantive codes relate to each other as hypotheses to be integrated into a theory. Glaser (1978) introduced 18 “coding families” (the Six Cs: Causes, Contexts, Contingencies, Consequences, Covariances, Conditions, plus families like “degree,” “interactive,” and “identity-self”). These codes weave the fractured story back together—but must earn their way into the theory.

Charmaz’s Example: Forms of Telling

In analyzing Bonnie Presley and others’ interviews, Charmaz discovered that telling other people about having a chronic illness poses emotional and interactional dilemmas. She coded for a range between spontaneous statements and staged pronouncements, then linked forms of telling to the relative presence or absence of participants’ control. She found that the participant’s subjective stake in telling exceeded what a researcher could plot along a simple continuum—subjectivity and objectivity meet when participants flaunt illness due to unresolved problems of self-acceptance.
